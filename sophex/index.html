The Sophex seminar is a seminar run by first-year mathematics graduate students at UT Austin that is designed to be a safe space for first-year students to practice speaking and to facilitate getting to know each other. This is achieved by only letting first and second-year students attend talks. Below are the talks for the 2025-2026 academic year.

For the 2025-2026 academic year, the Sophex seminar meets on Fridays from 4:00 PM to 5:00 PM in PMA 12.166 (12th floor classroom), followed by dinner at a nearby location.

If you would like to present a talk in Spring 2026, please email the Sophex seminar organizers Justin Le (justinle[at]utexas.edu) or Kazi Aryan Amin (aryanamin[at]utexas.edu).

September 5:   Kazi Aryan Amin

Horror movies that changed the genre

I'll be talking about movies which mark inflection points in horror history. These, while not necessarily being the 'best to do it', either introduced a new trope, sparked movements, or broadened audience expectations in a new aspect, and in general heavily influenced movies that came after them.

September 12: Viktoria Ipatenkova

On a Sufficient Condition for Chair Vacancy: A Dynamical Systems Analysis of Auditory-Stimulated Locomotion in Homo sapiens

This presentation will explore a conjectured sufficient condition for the non-equilibrium state of a human subject relative to a seated position—a phenomenon colloquially known as "you won't sit on your chair." We will formally posit that the auditory input of a specific class of music, herein defined as "popular music" or "pop," serves as a sufficient trigger for this state transition. The talk will model the human postural control system as a dynamical system where the application of this auditory stimulus acts as a forcing function, destabilizing the system's equilibrium (sitting) and inducing a phase transition into a non-stationary, rhythmic state (dancing). Our analysis will proceed chronologically, tracing the topological evolution of this forcing function's parameters—melody, rhythm, and harmonic structure—from its foundational axioms in the mid-20th century to its complex manifestations in the 2010s. The talk will conclude with an interactive segment applying our model to a series of case studies from the 2010s. The audience will be challenged to identify the artist based solely on a minimal set of sonic features, testing the robustness of our proposed classification metrics. Furthermore, we will examine edge cases and pathological examples—so-called "cringe" states—where the system's response deviates from the predicted attractor, leading to unstable or chaotic behavioral outputs.
